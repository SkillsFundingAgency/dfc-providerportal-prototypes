About this job

Large goods vehicle (LGV) and heavy goods vehicle (HGV) lorry drivers transport and deliver goods between suppliers and customers.

In this role, you’ll provide excellent customer service to promote your company, and make sure companies and individuals get the goods they need.

Typical duties

In a typical day as an LGV driver, you could:

  • plan delivery schedules and routes with transport managers
  • check your vehicle to identify and report any issues
  • secure loads safely on the vehicle
  • supervise or help to load and unload goods
  • complete delivery paperwork and log books

You always need to drive safely within the law and keep your vehicle tidy and roadworthy.

You may also follow traffic reports and change your route if necessary.

Salary, working hours and environment

Salary

Your salary may vary depending on your experience. You could expect:

  • a starting salary of £19,000
  • an experienced salary of up to £36,000

Working hours

You may work between 37 and 39 hours a week. Long journeys and international travel may require you to work unsocial hours and weekends.

Working environment

Your working environment may be physically active and you'll travel often.

Skills needed

As an LGV driver, you’ll need to be able to:

  • concentrate and stay focused while driving
  • carry out basic tasks on a computer or hand-held device like a phone
  • communicate with lots of different people and deliver excellent customer service skills
  • organise your time and workload
  • remain calm in stressful situations
  • use physical skills to lift, bend and carry heavy goods

You’ll also need to develop specialist skills such as knowledge of vehicle and public safety and security, and a knowledge of transport methods, costs and benefits.

Extra requirements:

You’ll need to:

  • be over 18 years of age
  • have a driving licence that includes LGV for lorries, or PCV for buses, if you work with these vehicles
  • have a Certificate of Professional Competence (CPC) - this is known as the Driver CPC

Every 5 years, you’ll need to take 35 hours of driver CPC training and renew your lorry driving licence.

How to become an LGV driver


You can get into this job through:

  • an apprenticeship
  • on the job training
  • specialist training courses

Apprenticeship

You can get into this job through a large goods vehicle (LGV) driver C and E intermediate apprenticeship.

The armed forces have apprenticeship roles for drivers including for heavy goods vehicles.

You’ll usually need some GCSEs, usually including English and maths, or equivalent, for an intermediate apprenticeship.

On the job training

If you're already working for a transport operator, for example as a clerk, your employer may be willing to put you through training to get your large goods vehicle licence. Find jobs near you

Specialist training courses

You'll need to get a professional driving qualification called the Driver Certificate of Professional Competence (CPC).

There is information on some of the vocational qualifications you can take on find training to become an LGV driver.

You can find training providers in your region by searching online. The Joint Approvals Unit for Periodic Training lists providers of refresher training for qualified LGV drivers. Many of these also offer initial LGV training. The National Vocational Driving Instructor Register (NVDIR) also contains a list of registered LGV training centres.

You may also be able to apply for a place on a Skills Bootcamp in LGV driving. Skills Bootcamps are free, flexible courses that last up to 16 weeks. If you successfully complete the course you will be guaranteed a job interview with a local employer. You'll be fully trained to drive large vehicles, and will gain a category C or category CE licence.

Career tips

Companies often need more LGV drivers at peak times. Demand for jobs can affect salaries.

Look for seasonal opportunities on retail and delivery company websites and with specialist driver recruitment agencies.

Future progression

You can often increase your salary through shift allowances and bonuses.

You could take further training and gain an ADR (Advisory Dangerous Goods by Road) certificate to drive hazardous goods like toxic chemicals by tanker.

With experience, you could train to become an LGV instructor, freight transport planner or move into management.

You could become a self-employed LGV driver and work for different companies or set up your own transport company.
